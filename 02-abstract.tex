\chap{Abstract} 

Prediction-powered inference studies how machine-learning predictions can support valid statistical inference when gold-standard labels are available only on a limited calibration subset. This thesis examines that problem from three connected angles: efficient correction with noisy surrogate labels, design-stage planning for prediction-powered studies, and a protein--protein interaction application in which prediction and inference are linked through a frozen surrogate model. The first methodological foundation revisits recent work on noisy LLM-as-a-judge evaluation and places prediction-powered correction in the broader literature on misclassification correction, surrogate-assisted estimation, and semiparametric efficiency. The second foundation summarizes recent prediction-powered power-analysis results, with emphasis on how surrogate quality enters asymptotic variance and labeled-sample planning through pilot \(\rho^2\).

The applied part of the thesis develops a leakage-aware protein-edge prediction workflow using processed Intra and BIOGRID-human benchmarks, per-protein sequence and text embeddings, pairwise feature construction, and neural prediction heads. The strongest surrogate obtained in this study is a frozen three-seed attention ensemble built from sequence and UniProt text representations. That ensemble is then used as the prediction layer for downstream PPI and \texttt{PPI++} estimation. Empirically, corrected estimates remain close to their full-data references across the main held-out test splits even with small calibration budgets, indicating that the surrogate is strong enough to support stable downstream correction. The published i.i.d. planning formulas also provide a useful baseline in this setting, but the protein application shows that apparent sample-size gains should be interpreted cautiously because shared-protein edges violate strict independence. To address this gap, the thesis adds a dependence-sensitive design-effect analysis rather than treating the i.i.d. theory as universally valid.

Taken together, the thesis argues that prediction-powered inference is best viewed not only as a post hoc correction device, but as a broader workflow linking surrogate construction, calibration, and study planning.
