\chapter{Protein Results, PPI/\texttt{PPI++}, and Dependence Sensitivity} \label{chap:protein_results}

This chapter reports the protein-specific empirical results. The discussion is organized in three layers. First, it summarizes the predictive performance of the actual mainline surrogate. Second, it shows how the canonical exported predictions are passed into PPI/\texttt{PPI++} estimation. Third, it examines power/sample-size planning under the published i.i.d. formulas and then adds a dependence-sensitive extension motivated by shared-protein edges.

\section{Prediction Layer: Mainline Performance}

The strongest current surrogate in the repository is the sequence-plus-text ESM2-3B attention ensemble introduced in Chapter~\ref{chap:protein_pipeline}. Its performance on the two main test splits is:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Intra test}: PR-AUC 0.715 and ROC-AUC 0.722.
    \item \textbf{BIOGRID-human test}: PR-AUC 0.799 and ROC-AUC 0.810.
\end{enumerate}
Relative to the best single attention seed, the ensemble improves stability and ranking performance; relative to the best MLP baseline, it provides a modest but consistent gain. The main empirical lesson is therefore not that attention radically changes the task, but that a strong prediction head plus seed averaging produces the most reliable surrogate for the downstream inference layer.

This interpretation is consistent with the recent PPI-prediction literature. Deep sequence models and protein-language-model-based approaches can achieve strong within-dataset performance, but their apparent gains are strongly shaped by benchmark design, negative sampling, and leakage control \parencite{dscript2021, xcapt52024, park2012, bernett2024}. For that reason, the current thesis treats the predictive pipeline primarily as a source of a high-quality surrogate for downstream inference, not as a claim of universal state-of-the-art performance against every published PPI benchmark.

\section{Inference Layer: Canonical Export and PPI/\texttt{PPI++}}

All inference in this thesis is driven by the canonical prediction export rather than the raw training outputs. The handoff is:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item per-run \texttt{predictions.csv} from neural training,
    \item ensemble-averaged predictions from \texttt{10\_ensemble\_predictions.py},
    \item canonical export from \texttt{07\_export\_predictions.py}, and
    \item R-based inference using \texttt{01\_run\_ppi\_estimators.R} and \texttt{02\_derive\_confusion\_metrics.R}.
\end{enumerate}

This separation mirrors the statistical distinction emphasized in the prediction-powered literature: prediction and inference are related, but they are not the same object \parencite{ppi_framework, ppi_pp, llmjudge_eif}. The predictor supplies a scalable surrogate, whereas the calibration set corrects the residual bias and enables uncertainty quantification.

Figure~\ref{fig:chap5_ppi_budget} shows the main PPI/\texttt{PPI++} summaries on the two test splits. Even at small calibration budgets, the estimated prevalence-, accuracy-, and error-type functionals remain close to the full-data references, which indicates that the frozen surrogate is strong enough to support stable correction with limited labels.

\section{Dependence Sensitivity for Shared-Protein Edges}

The published formulas above assume independent observations. Protein-edge data, however, are naturally graph-structured. If two edges share a protein, then their features, predicted scores, and even validation labels can be correlated. To make that issue explicit, this thesis adds a dependence-sensitive extension rather than silently treating the published formulas as universally correct. The concern is not purely abstract: the pair-input evaluation literature repeatedly shows that dependence across constituent objects can distort performance estimation if the benchmark design ignores it \parencite{park2012, bernett2024}.

Let an edge be indexed by $e = (i,j)$, and let $N(e)$ denote the set of other edges that share protein $i$ or $j$. For an edge-level influence contribution $\psi_e^{\lambda}$, the relevant large-sample variance under dependence is no longer purely diagonal. Cross-edge covariance terms need not vanish, and therefore the usual i.i.d. confidence intervals and sample-size calculations can be optimistic. In this first draft, I do not replace the published theorem. Instead, I report a sensitivity analysis based on a design effect,
\[
\mathrm{deff} \in \{1.00, 1.25, 1.50, 2.00\},
\]
which inflates the labeled-sample requirement relative to the i.i.d. baseline.

\subsection{Variance Decomposition Under Shared-Protein Dependence}

For a fixed tuning parameter $\lambda$, the mean-type prediction-powered estimator can be written as
\[
\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda}
=
\bar{Y}_{L} + \lambda(\bar{f}_{U} - \bar{f}_{L})
=
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{e \in L}(Y_e - \lambda f_e)
+
\frac{\lambda}{N}\sum_{e \in U} f_e,
\]
where $L$ is the labeled calibration set with size $n$ and $U$ is the prediction-only set with size $N$. Define the labeled residual term and prediction-only term by
\[
R_e^{\lambda} = Y_e - \lambda f_e,
\qquad
G_e^{\lambda} = \lambda f_e.
\]
Then
\[
\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda} = \bar{R}^{\lambda}_{L} + \bar{G}^{\lambda}_{U},
\]
so the variance decomposes as
\[
\Var(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda})
=
\Var(\bar{R}^{\lambda}_{L})
+
\Var(\bar{G}^{\lambda}_{U})
+
2\Cov(\bar{R}^{\lambda}_{L}, \bar{G}^{\lambda}_{U}).
\]

Under the i.i.d. approximation used in the published power formulas, off-diagonal covariances vanish and one recovers
\[
\Var_{\mathrm{iid}}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda})
\approx
\frac{\Var(R^{\lambda})}{n}
+
\frac{\Var(G^{\lambda})}{N}
=
\frac{\Var(Y-\lambda f)}{n}
+
\frac{\lambda^2 \Var(f)}{N}.
\]
Expanding the labeled residual term gives
\[
\Var(Y-\lambda f)
=
\Var(Y) + \lambda^2 \Var(f) - 2\lambda \Cov(Y,f).
\]

For graph-structured protein-edge data, however, the cross-edge covariance terms do not generally vanish. If $A_{ee'} = 1$ indicates that edges $e$ and $e'$ share at least one protein, then the dependence-aware variance can be written as
\[
\Var_{\mathrm{dep}}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda})
=
\frac{1}{n^2}\sum_{e,e' \in L}\Cov(R_e^{\lambda}, R_{e'}^{\lambda})
+
\frac{1}{N^2}\sum_{u,u' \in U}\Cov(G_u^{\lambda}, G_{u'}^{\lambda})
+
\frac{2}{nN}\sum_{e \in L}\sum_{u \in U}\Cov(R_e^{\lambda}, G_u^{\lambda}),
\]
where the off-diagonal summands are concentrated on edge pairs with $A_{ee'} = 1$. This makes the key point explicit: the estimator is unchanged, but the variance is inflated by shared-protein dependence.

\subsection{Design-Effect Approximation and Sample-Size Inflation}

To obtain a tractable first-order sensitivity analysis, suppose the main effect of dependence is to inflate the labeled residual component while leaving the prediction-only term approximately unchanged because $N$ is large. The goal is not to prove a new general dependent-edge theorem. Rather, the goal is to show that once the omitted covariance terms are summarized by a single nonnegative inflation factor, the sample-size inflation rule used in the code follows exactly.

Let
\[
D_{\lambda}
=
\Var_{\mathrm{dep}}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda})
-
\left\{
\frac{\Var(Y-\lambda f)}{n}
+
\frac{\lambda^2 \Var(f)}{N}
\right\}
\]
denote the excess variance beyond the published i.i.d. baseline. If $\Var(Y-\lambda f)>0$ and $D_{\lambda}\ge 0$, define the design effect by
\[
d_{\lambda}
=
1+\frac{n D_{\lambda}}{\Var(Y-\lambda f)}.
\]
This representation makes precise what the chapter means by a design effect: it is the scalar that absorbs the omitted dependence-induced covariance terms into the labeled residual variance.

\begin{theorem}[Design-effect representation and sample-size inflation]
\label{thm:deff-inflation}
Fix $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$ and suppose $\Var(Y-\lambda f)>0$. Let
\[
S^2
=
\left(
\frac{\Delta}{z_{1-\alpha/2} + z_{1-\beta}}
\right)^2
\]
for a two-sided Wald design targeting absolute difference $\Delta$ at significance level $\alpha$ and power $1-\beta$. Assume $D_{\lambda}\ge 0$ and $S^2 > \lambda^2 \Var(f)/N$. Then:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item the dependence-aware variance can be written exactly as
    \[
    \Var_{\mathrm{dep}}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda})
    =
    d_{\lambda}\frac{\Var(Y-\lambda f)}{n}
    +
    \frac{\lambda^2 \Var(f)}{N},
    \qquad d_{\lambda}\ge 1;
    \]
    \item the smallest \emph{real-valued} labeled sample size satisfying the Wald planning inequality
    \[
    \Var_{\mathrm{dep}}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda}) \le S^2
    \]
    is
    \[
    n_{\mathrm{req}}^{\mathrm{dep}}(\lambda; d_{\lambda})
    =
    \frac{
    d_{\lambda}\Var(Y-\lambda f)
    }{
    S^2 - \lambda^2 \Var(f)/N
    };
    \]
    \item if
    \[
    n_{\mathrm{req}}^{\mathrm{iid}}(\lambda)
    =
    \frac{
    \Var(Y-\lambda f)
    }{
    S^2 - \lambda^2 \Var(f)/N
    }
    \]
    denotes the corresponding i.i.d. planning target, then
    \[
    n_{\mathrm{req}}^{\mathrm{dep}}(\lambda; d_{\lambda})
    =
    d_{\lambda}\, n_{\mathrm{req}}^{\mathrm{iid}}(\lambda).
    \]
\end{enumerate}
If an integer sample size is required in practice, one takes the ceiling of the real-valued expression above.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By the definition of $D_{\lambda}$,
\[
\Var_{\mathrm{dep}}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda})
=
\frac{\Var(Y-\lambda f)}{n}
+
\frac{\lambda^2 \Var(f)}{N}
+
D_{\lambda}.
\]
Substituting
\[
D_{\lambda}
=
\frac{(d_{\lambda}-1)\Var(Y-\lambda f)}{n},
\]
which follows directly from the definition of $d_{\lambda}$, gives
\[
\Var_{\mathrm{dep}}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda})
=
d_{\lambda}\frac{\Var(Y-\lambda f)}{n}
+
\frac{\lambda^2 \Var(f)}{N}.
\]
Because $D_{\lambda}\ge 0$ and $\Var(Y-\lambda f)>0$, the same definition implies $d_{\lambda}\ge 1$, proving part (1).

For part (2), impose the Wald planning inequality
\[
d_{\lambda}\frac{\Var(Y-\lambda f)}{n}
+
\frac{\lambda^2 \Var(f)}{N}
\le S^2.
\]
Since $S^2 > \lambda^2 \Var(f)/N$, subtracting the prediction-only term preserves positivity:
\[
d_{\lambda}\frac{\Var(Y-\lambda f)}{n}
\le
S^2 - \lambda^2 \Var(f)/N.
\]
Multiplying both sides by $n$ and dividing by the positive quantity $S^2 - \lambda^2 \Var(f)/N$ yields
\[
n
\ge
\frac{
d_{\lambda}\Var(Y-\lambda f)
}{
S^2 - \lambda^2 \Var(f)/N
}.
\]
Hence the smallest real-valued $n$ satisfying the inequality is exactly
\[
n_{\mathrm{req}}^{\mathrm{dep}}(\lambda; d_{\lambda})
=
\frac{
d_{\lambda}\Var(Y-\lambda f)
}{
S^2 - \lambda^2 \Var(f)/N
},
\]
which proves part (2).

For part (3), set $d_{\lambda}=1$ in the same expression to obtain the i.i.d. target
\[
n_{\mathrm{req}}^{\mathrm{iid}}(\lambda)
=
\frac{
\Var(Y-\lambda f)
}{
S^2 - \lambda^2 \Var(f)/N
}.
\]
Factoring this term from the dependence-sensitive expression gives
\[
n_{\mathrm{req}}^{\mathrm{dep}}(\lambda; d_{\lambda})
=
d_{\lambda}\,
n_{\mathrm{req}}^{\mathrm{iid}}(\lambda),
\]
as claimed.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:deff-inflation} is intentionally modest. It does not claim that one scalar can capture every form of graph dependence. It proves a narrower statement: once the extra covariance induced by shared-protein dependence is represented by a nonnegative scalar inflation on the labeled residual term, the linear sample-size inflation rule used in the current sensitivity-analysis code is algebraically exact rather than heuristic.

\caption{Dependence-sensitive planning curves obtained by inflating the i.i.d. labeled-sample requirement with a design effect. The design effect is used as a sensitivity parameter for shared-protein edge dependence, with \texttt{deff = 1.00} corresponding to the published i.i.d. baseline.}

The substantive implication is straightforward. The i.i.d. formulas remain useful as a baseline, but they should be read as optimistic when shared-protein dependence is non-negligible. The stronger the local dependence, the less dramatic the apparent label savings from PPI/\texttt{PPI++} become. This is the main protein-specific extension of the chapter: the point estimator is unchanged, but the variance interpretation and planning conclusions become dependence-sensitive.

\section{Chapter Summary}

The protein results support a coherent three-layer message. First, the attention-ensemble branch is the strongest current surrogate in the repository. Second, the exported predictions from that surrogate are good enough that PPI/\texttt{PPI++} estimates remain stable under modest calibration budgets. Third, the published power formulas provide a useful baseline, but graph-induced dependence should be treated as a real caveat in PPI applications rather than ignored. Existing PPI-prediction papers are still useful points of comparison, but only when their evaluation protocols are interpreted with the same caution demanded by the pair-input benchmarking literature \parencite{lee2023, park2012, bernett2024}.
