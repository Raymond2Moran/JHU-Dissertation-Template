\chapter{Protein Results, PPI/\texttt{PPI++}, and Dependence Sensitivity} \label{chap:protein_results}

This chapter reports the protein-specific empirical results. Unless otherwise noted, I retain the global notation from Section~\ref{sec:global_notation}; when the unit is specialized to an interaction edge, I simply replace the generic index \(i\) by an edge index \(e = (u,v)\). The discussion is organized in three layers. First, it summarizes the predictive performance of the actual mainline surrogate. Second, it shows how the frozen surrogate scores are carried into PPI/\texttt{PPI++} estimation. Third, it examines power/sample-size planning under the published i.i.d. formulas and then adds a dependence-sensitive extension motivated by shared-protein edges.

\section[Prediction Layer]{Prediction Layer: Mainline Performance}

The strongest current surrogate in this study is the sequence-plus-text ESM2-3B attention ensemble introduced in Chapter~\ref{chap:protein_pipeline}. Table~\ref{tab:chap5_predictive_metrics} reports the main predictive metrics for the frozen model gradient used throughout the later planning analysis: a sequence-only logreg model, a sequence-only MLP, a sequence-plus-text MLP, the strongest current single-attention run, and the frozen attention ensemble.

\input{generated_tables/chap5_predictive_metrics.tex}

The ensemble is strongest on both main test splits and also improves on the representative single-attention run. Relative to the best MLP baseline, the gain is modest but consistent. The main empirical lesson is therefore not that attention radically changes the task, but that a strong prediction head plus seed averaging produces the most reliable surrogate for the downstream inference layer.

This interpretation is consistent with the recent PPI-prediction literature. Deep sequence models and protein-language-model-based approaches can achieve strong within-dataset performance, but their apparent gains are strongly shaped by benchmark design, negative sampling, and leakage control \parencite{dscript2021, xcapt52024, park2012, bernett2024}. For that reason, the current thesis treats the predictive pipeline primarily as a source of a high-quality surrogate for downstream inference, not as a claim of universal state-of-the-art performance against every published PPI benchmark.

\section[Inference Layer]{Inference Layer: Canonical Export and PPI/\texttt{PPI++}}

All inference in this thesis is driven by the frozen prediction set rather than by raw model outputs. Predictions are first stabilized through seed averaging, then standardized for inference, and only then passed to the correction layer. This separation mirrors the statistical distinction emphasized in the prediction-powered literature: prediction and inference are related, but they are not the same object \parencite{ppi_framework, ppi_pp, llmjudge_eif}. The predictor supplies a scalable surrogate, whereas the calibration set corrects the residual bias and enables uncertainty quantification.

Table~\ref{tab:chap5_ppi_core} gives a compact view of the main mean-type summaries on the two held-out test splits. Even at small calibration budgets, the estimated prevalence, accuracy, and Brier functionals remain close to the full-data references. That is the operational sign that the frozen surrogate is strong enough to support stable correction with limited labels. This surrogate-strength comparison is more informative than the failed follow-up tuning campaign. After the frozen ensemble was established, I also ran a dedicated rho-focused search over alternative losses, early-stopping criteria, hard-negative settings, and post-hoc calibration. That search did not produce a replacement surrogate that improved pilot $\rho^2$ enough to beat the frozen ensemble without sacrificing ranking performance. For the thesis, the right conclusion is therefore conservative: the existing model gradient is already enough to show the protein-side planning story, and the main leverage appears to come from representation quality and evaluation design rather than from small head-level tuning changes.

\section[Dependence Sensitivity]{Dependence Sensitivity for Shared-Protein Edges}

The published formulas above assume independent observations. Protein-edge data, however, are naturally graph-structured. If two edges share a protein, then their features, predicted scores, and even validation labels can be correlated. In that setting, two labeled edges need not contribute two fully independent pieces of information. If many labeled edges share proteins, then the raw labeled sample size can overstate the amount of independent information actually available for planning. The concern is not purely abstract: the pair-input evaluation literature repeatedly shows that dependence across constituent objects can distort performance estimation if the benchmark design ignores it \parencite{park2012, bernett2024}.

This section therefore adds a dependence-sensitive extension rather than silently treating the published formulas as universally correct. The main user-facing quantity is a design effect, written \(\mathrm{deff}\). In plain English, \(\mathrm{deff}\) is a penalty factor for non-independence. If the i.i.d.\ planning calculation says that \(n\) labeled edges are sufficient, then \(\mathrm{deff}>1\) says that those \(n\) edges behave like only
\[
n_{\mathrm{eff}} = \frac{n}{\mathrm{deff}}
\]
independent labeled edges. Equivalently, the relevant variance is inflated by the factor \(\mathrm{deff}\). For example, \(\mathrm{deff}=1.25\) means 25\% larger variance and about 80\% as much effective labeled information, \(\mathrm{deff}=1.50\) means 50\% larger variance and \(n_{\mathrm{eff}} = 2n/3\), and \(\mathrm{deff}=2.00\) means doubled variance and only half as many effective independent labels.

In statistical terms, the design effect is a variance-inflation factor relative to the published i.i.d.\ planning formulas. Throughout this section, I use \(\mathrm{deff}\) for the user-facing sensitivity parameter shown in figures and tables, and \(d_{\lambda}\) for its fixed-\(\lambda\) mathematical version inside the derivation below. When the unit is specialized from a generic index \(i\) to a protein edge, I write \(e = (u,v)\) for an edge between proteins \(u\) and \(v\), and later use notation such as \(Y_e\), \(f_e\), and \(\psi_e^{\lambda}\) for edge-level outcomes, surrogate scores, and influence contributions.

In this first draft, I do not attempt to estimate the true design effect of the protein graph from first principles. Instead, I report a sensitivity analysis based on
\[
\mathrm{deff} \in \{1.00, 1.25, 1.50, 2.00\},
\]
which spans no inflation, mild inflation, moderate inflation, and a conservative doubling of the i.i.d.\ variance. The purpose of this grid is not to assert that one of these values is already known to be correct for the protein graph. The purpose is to show how quickly the apparent planning gain would shrink if shared-protein dependence inflates uncertainty by a plausible multiplicative factor.

\subsection[Variance Under Shared-Protein Dependence]{Variance Decomposition Under Shared-Protein Dependence}

For a fixed tuning parameter $\lambda$, the mean-type prediction-powered estimator can be written as
\[
\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda}
=
\bar{Y}_{L} + \lambda(\bar{f}_{U} - \bar{f}_{L})
=
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{e \in L}(Y_e - \lambda f_e)
+
\frac{\lambda}{N}\sum_{e \in U} f_e,
\]
where $L$ is the labeled calibration set with size $n$ and $U$ is the prediction-only set with size $N$. Define the labeled residual term and prediction-only term by
\[
R_e^{\lambda} = Y_e - \lambda f_e,
\qquad
G_e^{\lambda} = \lambda f_e.
\]
Then
\[
\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda} = \bar{R}^{\lambda}_{L} + \bar{G}^{\lambda}_{U},
\]
so the variance decomposes as
\[
\Var(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda})
=
\Var(\bar{R}^{\lambda}_{L})
+
\Var(\bar{G}^{\lambda}_{U})
+
2\Cov(\bar{R}^{\lambda}_{L}, \bar{G}^{\lambda}_{U}).
\]

Under the i.i.d. approximation used in the published power formulas, off-diagonal covariances vanish and one recovers
\[
\Var_{\mathrm{iid}}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda})
\approx
\frac{\Var(R^{\lambda})}{n}
+
\frac{\Var(G^{\lambda})}{N}
=
\frac{\Var(Y-\lambda f)}{n}
+
\frac{\lambda^2 \Var(f)}{N}.
\]
Expanding the labeled residual term gives
\[
\Var(Y-\lambda f)
=
\Var(Y) + \lambda^2 \Var(f) - 2\lambda \Cov(Y,f).
\]
For graph-structured protein-edge data, however, the cross-edge covariance terms do not generally vanish. If $A_{ee'} = 1$ indicates that edges $e$ and $e'$ share at least one protein, then the dependence-aware variance can be written as
\[
\Var_{\mathrm{dep}}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda})
=
\frac{1}{n^2}\sum_{e,e' \in L}\Cov(R_e^{\lambda}, R_{e'}^{\lambda})
+
\frac{1}{N^2}\sum_{u,u' \in U}\Cov(G_u^{\lambda}, G_{u'}^{\lambda})
+
\frac{2}{nN}\sum_{e \in L}\sum_{u \in U}\Cov(R_e^{\lambda}, G_u^{\lambda}),
\]
where the off-diagonal summands are concentrated on edge pairs with $A_{ee'} = 1$. This makes the key point explicit: the estimator is unchanged, but the variance is inflated by shared-protein dependence. The design effect is a compact way to summarize the contribution of those extra covariance terms when one does not want to model the full dependence graph explicitly.

\subsection[Design Effect and Sample Size]{Design-Effect Approximation and Sample-Size Inflation}

To obtain a tractable first-order sensitivity analysis, suppose the main effect of dependence is to inflate the labeled residual component while leaving the prediction-only term approximately unchanged because $N$ is large. This approximation has an immediate effective-sample-size interpretation. If dependence reduces the information content of the labeled sample from \(n\) independent edges to an effective sample size \(n_{\mathrm{eff},\lambda} = n/d_{\lambda}\), then
\[
\frac{\Var(Y-\lambda f)}{n_{\mathrm{eff},\lambda}}
=
d_{\lambda}\frac{\Var(Y-\lambda f)}{n}.
\]
So the same quantity can be read in two equivalent ways: either dependence inflates the labeled residual variance by \(d_{\lambda}\), or it shrinks the effective number of independent labeled edges to \(n/d_{\lambda}\). The goal is not to prove a new general dependent-edge theorem. Rather, the goal is to show that once the omitted covariance terms are summarized by a single nonnegative inflation factor, the sample-size inflation rule used in the code follows exactly.

Let
\[
D_{\lambda}
=
\Var_{\mathrm{dep}}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda})
-
\left\{
\frac{\Var(Y-\lambda f)}{n}
+
\frac{\lambda^2 \Var(f)}{N}
\right\}
\]
denote the excess variance beyond the published i.i.d. baseline. If $\Var(Y-\lambda f)>0$ and $D_{\lambda}\ge 0$, define the design effect by
\[
d_{\lambda}
=
1+\frac{n D_{\lambda}}{\Var(Y-\lambda f)}.
\]
This representation makes precise what the chapter means by a design effect: it is the scalar that absorbs the omitted dependence-induced covariance terms into the labeled residual variance.

\begin{theorem}[Design-effect representation and sample-size inflation]
\label{thm:deff-inflation}
Fix $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$ and suppose $\Var(Y-\lambda f)>0$. Let
\[
S^2
=
\left(
\frac{\Delta}{z_{1-\alpha/2} + z_{1-\beta}}
\right)^2
\]
for a two-sided Wald design targeting absolute difference $\Delta$ at significance level $\alpha$ and power $1-\beta$. Assume $D_{\lambda}\ge 0$ and $S^2 > \lambda^2 \Var(f)/N$. Then:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item the dependence-aware variance can be written exactly as
    \[
    \Var_{\mathrm{dep}}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda})
    =
    d_{\lambda}\frac{\Var(Y-\lambda f)}{n}
    +
    \frac{\lambda^2 \Var(f)}{N},
    \qquad d_{\lambda}\ge 1;
    \]
    \item the smallest \emph{real-valued} labeled sample size satisfying the Wald planning inequality
    \[
    \Var_{\mathrm{dep}}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda}) \le S^2
    \]
    is
    \[
    n_{\mathrm{req}}^{\mathrm{dep}}(\lambda; d_{\lambda})
    =
    \frac{
    d_{\lambda}\Var(Y-\lambda f)
    }{
    S^2 - \lambda^2 \Var(f)/N
    };
    \]
    \item if
    \[
    n_{\mathrm{req}}^{\mathrm{iid}}(\lambda)
    =
    \frac{
    \Var(Y-\lambda f)
    }{
    S^2 - \lambda^2 \Var(f)/N
    }
    \]
    denotes the corresponding i.i.d. planning target, then
    \[
    n_{\mathrm{req}}^{\mathrm{dep}}(\lambda; d_{\lambda})
    =
    d_{\lambda}\, n_{\mathrm{req}}^{\mathrm{iid}}(\lambda).
    \]
\end{enumerate}
If an integer sample size is required in practice, one takes the ceiling of the real-valued expression above.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By the definition of $D_{\lambda}$,
\[
\Var_{\mathrm{dep}}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda})
=
\frac{\Var(Y-\lambda f)}{n}
+
\frac{\lambda^2 \Var(f)}{N}
+
D_{\lambda}.
\]
Substituting
\[
D_{\lambda}
=
\frac{(d_{\lambda}-1)\Var(Y-\lambda f)}{n},
\]
which follows directly from the definition of $d_{\lambda}$, gives
\[
\Var_{\mathrm{dep}}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda})
=
d_{\lambda}\frac{\Var(Y-\lambda f)}{n}
+
\frac{\lambda^2 \Var(f)}{N}.
\]
Because $D_{\lambda}\ge 0$ and $\Var(Y-\lambda f)>0$, the same definition implies $d_{\lambda}\ge 1$, proving part (1).

For part (2), impose the Wald planning inequality
\[
d_{\lambda}\frac{\Var(Y-\lambda f)}{n}
+
\frac{\lambda^2 \Var(f)}{N}
\le S^2.
\]
Since $S^2 > \lambda^2 \Var(f)/N$, subtracting the prediction-only term preserves positivity:
\[
d_{\lambda}\frac{\Var(Y-\lambda f)}{n}
\le
S^2 - \lambda^2 \Var(f)/N.
\]
Multiplying both sides by $n$ and dividing by the positive quantity $S^2 - \lambda^2 \Var(f)/N$ yields
\[
n
\ge
\frac{
d_{\lambda}\Var(Y-\lambda f)
}{
S^2 - \lambda^2 \Var(f)/N
}.
\]
Hence the smallest real-valued $n$ satisfying the inequality is exactly
\[
n_{\mathrm{req}}^{\mathrm{dep}}(\lambda; d_{\lambda})
=
\frac{
d_{\lambda}\Var(Y-\lambda f)
}{
S^2 - \lambda^2 \Var(f)/N
},
\]
which proves part (2).

For part (3), set $d_{\lambda}=1$ in the same expression to obtain the i.i.d. target
\[
n_{\mathrm{req}}^{\mathrm{iid}}(\lambda)
=
\frac{
\Var(Y-\lambda f)
}{
S^2 - \lambda^2 \Var(f)/N
}.
\]
Factoring this term from the dependence-sensitive expression gives
\[
n_{\mathrm{req}}^{\mathrm{dep}}(\lambda; d_{\lambda})
=
d_{\lambda}\,
n_{\mathrm{req}}^{\mathrm{iid}}(\lambda),
\]
as claimed.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:deff-inflation} is intentionally modest. It does not claim that one scalar can capture every form of graph dependence. It proves a narrower statement: once the extra covariance induced by shared-protein dependence is represented by a nonnegative scalar inflation on the labeled residual term, the linear sample-size inflation rule used in the current sensitivity-analysis code is algebraically exact rather than heuristic.

The plain-language interpretation is straightforward. If \(d_{\lambda}=1.50\), then the labeled residual variance is 50\% larger than the i.i.d.\ formula suggests, and \(n\) labeled edges behave like only \(n/1.50\) independent edges for the purpose of this planning calculation. That is why the sensitivity curves move upward as \(\mathrm{deff}\) increases: the estimator is not being changed, but the same nominal labeled sample is treated as carrying less effective information.

The substantive implication is straightforward. The i.i.d. formulas remain useful as a baseline, but they should be read as optimistic when shared-protein dependence is non-negligible. The stronger the local dependence, the less dramatic the apparent label savings from PPI/\texttt{PPI++} become. This is the main protein-specific extension of the chapter: the point estimator is unchanged, but the variance interpretation and planning conclusions become dependence-sensitive.

\section{Chapter Summary}

The protein results support a coherent three-layer message. First, the attention-ensemble branch is the strongest current surrogate in the repository. Second, the exported predictions from that surrogate are good enough that PPI/\texttt{PPI++} estimates remain stable under modest calibration budgets. Third, the published power formulas provide a useful baseline, but graph-induced dependence should be treated as a real caveat in PPI applications rather than ignored. Existing PPI-prediction papers are still useful points of comparison, but only when their evaluation protocols are interpreted with the same caution demanded by the pair-input benchmarking literature \parencite{lee2023, park2012, bernett2024}.
