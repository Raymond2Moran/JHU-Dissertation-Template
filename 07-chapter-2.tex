\chapter{Prior Work I: Efficient Inference for Noisy LLM-as-a-Judge Evaluation} \label{chap:foundation_llm}

This chapter summarizes the first methodological foundation used in the thesis, the preprint \emph{Efficient Inference for Noisy LLM-as-a-Judge Evaluation} \parencite{llmjudge_eif}. It should be read as a direct extension of the original prediction-powered inference line: the starting point is the PPI/\texttt{PPI++} estimator family, and the new question is what happens when the cheap label is itself noisy, biased, or better interpreted as a surrogate outcome rather than as a clean prediction target. Although the paper is motivated by LLM-as-a-judge evaluation, its core statistical structure is directly relevant here: a large set of cheap surrogate labels is available for many units, while a smaller calibration set contains both the surrogate and the gold-standard outcome.

\section{Global Statistical Setup and Notation}
\label{sec:global_notation}

This section fixes the notation used throughout the remainder of the thesis. The same symbols are reused in the methodological foundation chapters and in the later protein-edge application; what changes from chapter to chapter is the scientific interpretation of a ``unit'', not the statistical structure.

\paragraph{Generic unit-level notation.}
Index generic units by \(i\). Let \(X_i\) denote the raw input or feature vector associated with unit \(i\), let \(Y_i\) denote the gold-standard outcome of interest, and let \(\widehat{Y}_i\) denote a cheap observed surrogate label when such a surrogate is directly recorded. In the LLM-as-a-judge setting of \textcite{llmjudge_eif}, \(\widehat{Y}_i\) is the cheap judge label itself. In later chapters, I use the shorthand \(f_i = f(X_i)\) for a generic model-based surrogate prediction. In the protein application, the unit is usually an edge and \(f_i\) is the predicted interaction score.

\paragraph{Observed-label indicator and sample split.}
Let \(R_i \in \{0,1\}\) indicate whether the gold-standard label \(Y_i\) is observed, and write \(\pi = \Pr(R_i = 1)\). The data are partitioned into
\begin{enumerate}
    \item a labeled calibration set \(L = \{i : R_i = 1\}\) of size \(n\), on which both the gold label and the surrogate are observed, and
    \item a large prediction-only set \(U = \{i : R_i = 0\}\) of size \(N\), on which the surrogate is available at scale.
\end{enumerate}
When the later power-analysis chapters speak of a pilot set, calibration set, or labeled subset, they refer to this same object \(L\) unless otherwise stated. Similarly, the evaluation population or unlabeled prediction pool is represented by \(U\).

\paragraph{Averages and target parameter.}
For any quantity \(A_i\), write
\[
\overline{A}_{L} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i \in L} A_i,
\qquad
\overline{A}_{U} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i \in U} A_i.
\]
The primary mean-type target in the thesis is
\[
\theta = \mathbb{E}[Y_i].
\]
For a binary outcome, \(\theta\) is a prevalence or positive-rate parameter; for a continuous outcome, \(\theta\) is an ordinary mean. This convention will be retained in the power-analysis and protein-application chapters.

\paragraph{Calibration and surrogate notation.}
When the cheap label is treated through an explicit regression or calibration function, write
\[
\mu(\widehat{Y}_i) = \mathbb{E}[Y_i \mid \widehat{Y}_i].
\]
When the surrogate is model-based rather than directly observed, I use \(f_i = f(X_i)\) as the generic prediction notation. With that convention, the same estimator formulas can be written without changing statistical meaning when moving from noisy judge labels to protein-edge prediction scores.

\paragraph{Prediction-powered estimator family.}
In the mean-setting, the original PPI estimator is
\[
\widehat{\theta}_{\mathrm{PPI}}
=
\overline{f}_{U}
+
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i \in L}(Y_i-f_i),
\]
and the one-parameter \texttt{PPI++} family is
\[
\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda}
=
\overline{Y}_{L}
+
\lambda(\overline{f}_{U}-\overline{f}_{L}).
\]
Here \(\lambda\) is the tuning parameter that controls how strongly the prediction-only sample enters the corrected estimator. Later chapters distinguish three common cases:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item vanilla PPI, corresponding to \(\lambda = 1\);
    \item estimated-lambda \texttt{PPI++}, where \(\hat{\lambda}\) is estimated from pilot or calibration data; and
    \item oracle-lambda \texttt{HPPI++}, where \(\lambda\) is chosen to minimize the working asymptotic variance.
\end{enumerate}

This setup can also be viewed as a missing-data problem: the target outcome \(Y_i\) is observed only on the labeled subset, while the surrogate is observed for everyone \parencite{rubin1976, tsiatis2006, robins1995}. The paper compares two broad correction strategies. The first treats \(\widehat{Y}\) as a mismeasured version of \(Y\) and uses direct measurement-error correction, such as Rogan--Gladen-style estimators. The second treats \(\widehat{Y}\) or \(f(X)\) as a generic surrogate outcome and uses prediction-powered residual correction. The central message is that these views can be unified through efficient influence functions and semiparametric efficiency \parencite{rogan_gladen, eif_ref, tsiatis2006, llmjudge_eif}.

\section{Relationship to Earlier Statistical Literature}

The conceptual roots of this chapter lie in three older literatures. The first is the literature on imperfect diagnostics and measurement error, where the observed label is a noisy proxy for an unobserved truth. Rogan--Gladen-style prevalence correction is a classical example of this perspective \parencite{rogan_gladen}. The second is the missing-data literature, which studies how to use partially observed outcomes together with auxiliary variables and sampling indicators \parencite{rubin1976, robins1995, tsiatis2006}. The third is semiparametric efficiency theory, where efficient influence functions characterize the best possible first-order use of auxiliary information under a given model \parencite{eif_ref, tsiatis2006}. The LLM-as-a-judge paper is important because it connects these traditions to modern AI-assisted evaluation problems rather than treating them as separate worlds.

That connection matters for the rest of the thesis. In protein-edge prediction, the cheap label is not a binary judge produced by an LLM but a model-based prediction score for an interaction edge. Even so, the statistical skeleton is the same: the inexpensive signal is observed broadly, the expensive signal is observed only on a smaller labeled subset, and the inferential problem is how to combine them efficiently.

\section{Why This Paper Matters for the Thesis}

The importance of \textcite{llmjudge_eif} for this thesis is conceptual rather than domain-specific. In the protein application, the surrogate is not an LLM judge score but a learned edge-prediction score produced by a neural model. The structure is nevertheless the same:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item the surrogate is available at scale,
    \item the gold label is expensive, and
    \item the calibration set is used to correct systematic error.
\end{enumerate}

The paper also clarifies a useful modeling distinction. A cheap surrogate can be treated as a noisy measurement, but it can also be treated as a generic prediction that is corrected through calibration. The latter perspective is more flexible for this thesis because the protein-edge surrogate is continuous, model-based, and not naturally parameterized by a simple sensitivity/specificity pair. That flexibility is one reason prediction-powered methods are a better fit for the protein application than a strict diagnostic-test abstraction.

\section{Efficient Estimation Viewpoint}

The cleanest way to see the relationship between the original PPI papers and the noisy-surrogate extension is to start from the estimator family itself. Using the generic surrogate notation $f_i$, prediction-powered inference can be written as a residual-correction estimator. In the simplest mean-setting,
\[
\widehat{\theta}_{\mathrm{PPI}} = \overline{f}_{U} + \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i \in L} \{Y_i - f_i\},
\]
where $f_i$ is the surrogate prediction, $L$ denotes the labeled calibration set, and $U$ denotes the large unlabeled set. \texttt{PPI++} introduces a tuning parameter $\lambda$,
\[
\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda} = \overline{Y}_{L} + \lambda\, (\overline{f}_{U} - \overline{f}_{L}),
\]
which can improve efficiency when the surrogate is strong \parencite{ppi_framework, ppi_pp}. The LLM-as-a-judge preprint keeps this same estimator family in view, but asks a sharper question: when the cheap label is noisy, when is the working choice $f_i\approx Y_i$ good enough, and when should one instead target the full regression function $\mu(\widehat{Y})=\mathbb{E}[Y\mid \widehat{Y}]$?

The contribution of \textcite{llmjudge_eif} is to place these estimators in an efficient-influence-function framework rooted in semiparametric missing-data theory \parencite{robins1995, tsiatis2006}. That perspective explains when simple correction rules are efficient, when they are suboptimal, and why a stronger surrogate typically leads to shorter confidence intervals after calibration. For the purposes of this thesis, the main takeaway is that better prediction quality helps twice: it improves point prediction, and it also improves downstream inferential efficiency.

\subsection{Key Formulas for Mean Estimation with a Surrogate Label}

The efficient-influence-function perspective can be written explicitly. Let $R$ be the indicator that the gold-standard label is observed, with $\Pr(R=1)=\pi$, and suppose $R$ is independent of $(Y,\widehat{Y})$. Define \(\mu(\widehat{Y}) = \mathbb{E}[Y \mid \widehat{Y}]\).
Then the efficient influence function for the mean target $\theta=\mathbb{E}[Y]$ takes the form
\[
\phi(O)
=
\mu(\widehat{Y}) - \theta
+
\frac{R}{\pi}\{Y-\mu(\widehat{Y})\},
\]
where $O=(R,RY,\widehat{Y})$. The corresponding efficient estimator therefore has the generic residual-correction form
\[
\widehat{\theta}(\mu)
=
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i \in U}\mu(\widehat{Y}_i)
+
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i \in L}\{Y_i-\mu(\widehat{Y}_i)\}.
\]
This equation is the bridge between the semiparametric theory and the practical estimators used later in the thesis. Standard PPI is obtained by the working choice $\mu(\widehat{Y}_i) \approx f_i$, whereas \texttt{PPI++} uses the linear approximation $\mu(\widehat{Y}_i) \approx \lambda f_i$ \parencite{ppi_framework, ppi_pp, llmjudge_eif}. In the binary judge setting, the simple special case $f_i=\widehat{Y}_i$ recovers the most familiar form of the estimator. Detailed algebra for these identities is summarized in Appendix~\ref{chap:appendix-a}.

\subsection{Binary Misclassification View and Rogan--Gladen}

The direct measurement-error view can also be written explicitly in the binary case. Let \(q_0 = \Pr(\widehat{Y}=0 \mid Y=0)\), \(q_1 = \Pr(\widehat{Y}=1 \mid Y=1)\), and \(p = \Pr(\widehat{Y}=1)\).
Then
\[
p
=
\Pr(\widehat{Y}=1 \mid Y=0)\Pr(Y=0)
+
\Pr(\widehat{Y}=1 \mid Y=1)\Pr(Y=1)
=
(1-q_0)(1-\theta)+q_1\theta,
\]
which simplifies to
\[
p = (q_0+q_1-1)\theta + (1-q_0).
\]
Solving for $\theta$ yields the Rogan--Gladen identity
\[
\theta = \frac{p+q_0-1}{q_0+q_1-1}.
\]
This is the classical correction formula against which residual-correction estimators such as PPI and \texttt{PPI++} are compared in the LLM-as-a-judge paper \parencite{rogan_gladen, llmjudge_eif}. In the protein chapters, I do not use this binary misclassification model directly, but it remains a useful conceptual reference point.
