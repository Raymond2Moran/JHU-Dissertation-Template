\chapter{Introduction} \label{chap:introduction}

Prediction-powered inference (PPI) starts from a now-common scientific constraint: modern machine-learning systems can generate cheap predictions at scale, but gold-standard labels remain expensive \parencite{ppi_framework}. The central statistical question is no longer just how to make predictions, but how to use those predictions to perform valid downstream inference. In the original PPI framework, predictions are treated as an auxiliary signal that is available broadly, while a smaller labeled subset is used to correct residual bias. This yields estimators that preserve validity while potentially using far fewer gold-standard labels than classical label-only analyses \parencite{ppi_framework}. \texttt{PPI++} sharpens this idea by introducing a tuning parameter $\lambda$ that optimally trades off labeled and prediction-only information, thereby improving efficiency when the surrogate is strong \parencite{ppi_pp}.

Throughout this thesis, the abbreviation PPI refers to \emph{prediction-powered inference}. To avoid ambiguity, the biological application is described explicitly as protein--protein interaction or protein-interaction prediction.

This thesis is organized around that methodological line of work. Its primary focus is not to introduce yet another biological prediction architecture in isolation, but to study how the PPI viewpoint can be extended and specialized. Two preprints form the main methodological extensions considered here. The first, \emph{Efficient Inference for Noisy LLM-as-a-Judge Evaluation}, studies how prediction-powered correction relates to classical misclassification correction and semiparametric efficiency when the cheap label is itself noisy and biased \parencite{llmjudge_eif}. The second, \emph{Power Analysis for Prediction-Powered Inference}, studies how predictor quality enters prospective design questions such as statistical power and required labeled sample size \parencite{ppi_power}. Together, these works extend the original PPI and \texttt{PPI++} program from post hoc correction toward a broader inferential workflow: surrogate construction, calibration, efficiency analysis, and design-stage planning.

The protein-interaction application enters this thesis as the main case study for those ideas, not as the sole conceptual starting point. Protein interactions are biologically important and experimentally expensive to validate, which makes them a natural setting for prediction-powered reasoning. Modern models can generate large numbers of candidate edge scores from sequence and annotation information, but definitive labels remain limited and heterogeneous across assays and databases \parencite{vidal2011, biogrid2019, luck2020, uniprot2025}. This is therefore a useful biostatistical setting in which to ask whether strong predictors can be converted into reliable inference and efficient study design, rather than only into better rankings.

The central claim of the thesis is therefore methodological. Prediction-powered inference should be treated not only as a post hoc correction device, but as a full workflow that links surrogate construction, calibration, and design-stage planning. In the protein-edge application studied here, that workflow is instantiated by a frozen sequence-plus-text attention ensemble, a canonical prediction export, prediction-powered estimation on limited labeled subsets, and an explicit sensitivity analysis for the shared-protein dependence that is absent from the published i.i.d.\ planning theory.

\section{Scientific and Methodological Context}

Two streams of literature motivate the thesis. The first is the statistical literature that leads to PPI. Classical work on imperfect diagnostics, measurement error, missing data, and semiparametric efficiency asked how to recover a target parameter when the observed quantity is only a proxy for truth or is available only on a subset of units \parencite{rogan_gladen, rubin1976, robins1995, eif_ref, tsiatis2006}. The prediction-powered line of work reformulates that question for modern AI/ML settings, where predictions are plentiful and gold-standard labels are scarce. The original PPI paper shows how residual correction can deliver valid inference using a large prediction-only sample and a much smaller labeled sample \parencite{ppi_framework}. \texttt{PPI++} then shows that the same setup admits an efficiency-improving tuning parameter, making the quality of the prediction explicitly relevant for statistical precision \parencite{ppi_pp}. The two preprints studied in this thesis build directly on that foundation by addressing noisy surrogate labels and prospective power analysis \parencite{llmjudge_eif, ppi_power}.

The second stream is the computational biology literature on protein-interaction resources and prediction. Protein interactions mediate core cellular mechanisms, but interactome maps remain incomplete and difficult to validate experimentally at scale \parencite{vidal2011, rao2014, luck2020}. Curated resources such as BioGRID and UniProt are therefore central inputs for both model construction and downstream biological interpretation \parencite{biogrid2019, uniprot2025}. On the predictive side, protein-interaction modeling has moved from hand-engineered features toward deep sequence-based models, structure-aware methods, and protein language model embeddings \parencite{sun2017, dscript2021, prottrans2022, lin2023, lee2023}. This literature provides the application layer for the thesis, but the central thesis contribution remains methodological: prediction-powered inference and its extensions are the main organizing principle.

\section{Why Protein-Interaction Prediction Is a Good Test Case}

The protein application is a useful stress test for prediction-powered ideas for three reasons. First, the labels are meaningfully expensive: even curated resources are incomplete and heterogeneous because they summarize many assays, organisms, and validation conditions \parencite{biogrid2019, vidal2011}. Second, the features are naturally multimodal. Protein sequences can be encoded by large protein language models such as ProtTrans or ESM-style models, while textual descriptions from UniProt provide an orthogonal annotation view \parencite{prottrans2022, lin2023, uniprot2025, sentence_bert2019, minilm2020}. Third, the units are not naturally i.i.d. in the strongest sense because two edges may share a protein, which creates a local graph dependence structure that is not part of the standard theory in current prediction-powered power-analysis work. Precisely because the application is statistically awkward, it is useful for testing where the PPI and \texttt{PPI++} theory transfers cleanly and where additional caution or sensitivity analysis is needed.

The application is also a good warning case for evaluation methodology. Pair-input prediction problems are particularly vulnerable to overly optimistic assessment when train and test sets share constituent objects or when negative examples are generated without adequate controls \parencite{park2012, bernett2024}. In the present study, this issue appears directly in the benchmark design: the internal Intra dataset comes with predefined negatives, whereas the BIOGRID-human benchmark requires sampled non-positive pairs, so negative-edge construction is part of the statistical problem definition rather than a peripheral preprocessing detail. That literature directly motivates the leakage-aware train/validation/test design used in this thesis. Rather than treating benchmark construction as a peripheral engineering detail, the thesis treats evaluation design as part of the statistical problem definition.

\section{Research Questions}

The thesis is organized around four questions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item How should the original PPI and \texttt{PPI++} framework be understood as part of a broader literature on surrogate-assisted inference, measurement error, and semiparametric efficiency?
    \item How should prediction-powered methods be extended when the cheap labels are themselves noisy or surrogate outcomes?
    \item How does predictor quality enter power and sample-size planning in prediction-powered inference?
    \item In protein--protein interaction prediction, can a strong surrogate model be built and then used as the prediction layer for calibrated PPI/\texttt{PPI++} inference under leakage-aware evaluation and shared-protein dependence sensitivity?
\end{enumerate}

\section{Main Contributions}

This thesis makes four main contributions.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item It places the thesis explicitly in the prediction-powered line initiated by \textcite{ppi_framework} and extended by \textcite{ppi_pp}, treating those papers as the main conceptual foundation.
    \item It develops two extension chapters around that foundation: one on efficient correction for noisy surrogate labels \parencite{llmjudge_eif} and one on prediction-powered power analysis \parencite{ppi_power}.
    \item It develops a protein-edge prediction workflow built around processed Intra and BIOGRID edge tables, per-protein sequence and text representations, pairwise feature construction, merged training-pool construction, neural edge predictors, and seed ensembling. The predictive design is informed by the modern literature on sequence-based protein-interaction prediction, protein language modeling, and leakage-aware evaluation \parencite{dscript2021, prottrans2022, lin2023, park2012, bernett2024}.
    \item It uses the frozen best surrogate, a sequence-plus-text ESM2-3B attention ensemble, as the default prediction layer for PPI/\texttt{PPI++} estimation and labeled-budget analysis, and then adds a protein-specific dependence sensitivity analysis to the published i.i.d. power formulas.
\end{enumerate}

Chapter~\ref{chap:foundation_llm} summarizes the preprint \emph{Efficient Inference for Noisy LLM-as-a-Judge Evaluation} \parencite{llmjudge_eif} and places it in the broader literature on surrogate-label correction, measurement error, and efficient estimation. Chapter~\ref{chap:foundation_power} summarizes the preprint \emph{Power Analysis for Prediction-Powered Inference} \parencite{ppi_power}, focusing on asymptotic variance, the role of $\lambda$, and labeled-sample planning under an i.i.d. design.
