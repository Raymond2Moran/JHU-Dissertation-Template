\chapter{Introduction} \label{chap:introduction}

Prediction-powered inference (PPI) arises from a familiar constraint in modern empirical work: machine-learning systems can generate cheap predictions at scale, while gold-standard labels remain expensive \parencite{ppi_framework}. The central statistical question is therefore no longer only how to make predictions, but how to use those predictions for valid downstream inference. In the original PPI framework, predictions are treated as an auxiliary signal that is widely available, while a smaller labeled subset is used to correct residual bias. This yields estimators that retain validity while potentially requiring far fewer gold-standard labels than a classical label-only analysis \parencite{ppi_framework}. \texttt{PPI++} sharpens this idea by introducing a tuning parameter $\lambda$ that optimally trades off labeled and prediction-only information, thereby improving efficiency when the surrogate is strong \parencite{ppi_pp}.

Throughout this thesis, the abbreviation PPI refers to \emph{prediction-powered inference}. To avoid ambiguity, the biological application is described explicitly as protein--protein interaction or protein-interaction prediction.

This thesis is organized around that methodological line of work. Its aim is not to introduce a biological prediction architecture in isolation, but to examine how the PPI viewpoint extends to surrogate construction, calibration, and planning. Two recent preprints provide the main methodological extensions considered here. The first, \emph{Efficient Inference for Noisy LLM-as-a-Judge Evaluation}, studies how prediction-powered correction relates to classical misclassification correction and semiparametric efficiency when the cheap label is itself noisy and biased \parencite{llmjudge_eif}. The second, \emph{Power Analysis for Prediction-Powered Inference}, studies how predictor quality enters prospective design questions such as statistical power and required labeled sample size \parencite{ppi_power}. Taken together, these works extend the original PPI and \texttt{PPI++} program from post hoc correction toward a broader inferential workflow that includes surrogate construction, calibration, efficiency analysis, and design-stage planning.

The protein-interaction application serves as the main case study for these ideas, but it is not the thesis's sole conceptual starting point. Protein interactions are biologically important and experimentally expensive to validate, which makes them a natural setting for prediction-powered reasoning. Modern models can generate large numbers of candidate edge scores from sequence and annotation information, but definitive labels remain limited and heterogeneous across assays and databases \parencite{vidal2011, biogrid2019, luck2020, uniprot2025}. The application is therefore well suited to asking whether strong predictors can be turned into reliable inference and efficient study design, rather than only into better rankings.

The central claim of the thesis is therefore methodological. Prediction-powered inference should be treated not only as a post hoc correction device, but as a full workflow linking surrogate construction, calibration, and design-stage planning. In the protein-edge application studied here, that workflow is instantiated through a frozen sequence-plus-text attention ensemble, a canonical prediction export, prediction-powered estimation on limited labeled subsets, and an explicit sensitivity analysis for the shared-protein dependence that is absent from the published i.i.d.\ planning theory.

\section[Related Work]{Related Work and Thesis Positioning}

\subsection[PPI and Efficiency]{Prediction-Powered Inference and Efficient Estimation}

The statistical backbone of the thesis can be traced to earlier work on imperfect diagnostics, measurement error, missing data, and semiparametric efficiency. Those literatures asked how to recover a target parameter when the observed quantity is only a proxy for truth or when the target outcome is available only on a subset of units \parencite{rogan_gladen, rubin1976, robins1995, eif_ref, tsiatis2006}. The original PPI framework recasts that question for modern prediction systems: cheap predictions are available broadly, gold-standard labels are scarce, and the inferential goal is to combine the two without giving up validity \parencite{ppi_framework}. \texttt{PPI++} then makes surrogate quality enter the problem more explicitly through a tuning parameter that can improve efficiency when the predictor is strong \parencite{ppi_pp}.

This thesis sits within that line of work, but it does not stop with the original estimator family. The noisy-surrogate extension developed in \textcite{llmjudge_eif} is important here because it clarifies how prediction-powered correction relates to classical misclassification correction and to efficient-influence-function arguments when the cheap label is itself noisy or biased. That perspective matters beyond LLM evaluation. It provides the conceptual language used later in the thesis to interpret protein-edge prediction scores as inferential surrogates rather than as stand-alone benchmark outputs.

\subsection[Power and Planning]{Prediction-Powered Planning and Power Analysis}

Most of the early prediction-powered literature is retrospective: given a predictor and a labeled subset, how should one estimate a target parameter efficiently? The planning literature asks the prospective version of the same question: before labels are collected, how does surrogate quality translate into statistical power and required labeled sample size? \textcite{ppi_power} provides the main answer used in this thesis by showing, in the one-sample i.i.d.\ setting, how surrogate--truth agreement, summarized by \(\rho^2\), enters the asymptotic variance and the resulting sample-size formulas.

This design perspective is central to the thesis because it moves prediction-powered methods from post hoc correction to study planning. Chapter~\ref{chap:foundation_power} treats the published i.i.d.\ formulas as the baseline theory. The later protein chapters then ask a narrower but practically important question: how far can those formulas be carried into a graph-structured biological setting, where local dependence between edges is plausible and should therefore be handled explicitly rather than ignored?

\subsection[PPI Prediction]{Protein-Interaction Prediction and Benchmark Design}

The biological application draws on a separate literature in computational biology on interactome resources and protein-interaction prediction. Protein interactions mediate core cellular mechanisms, but interactome maps remain incomplete and difficult to validate experimentally at scale \parencite{vidal2011, rao2014, luck2020}. Curated resources such as BioGRID and UniProt are therefore essential both for building datasets and for interpreting predicted interactions downstream \parencite{biogrid2019, uniprot2025}. On the predictive side, the field has moved from hand-engineered features toward deep sequence models, protein language model embeddings, and hybrid architectures that combine multiple information sources \parencite{sun2017, dscript2021, prottrans2022, lin2023, lee2023}.

That literature is informative for this thesis in two ways. First, it shows that strong single-protein representations are now practically attainable. Second, it shows that benchmark design can dominate apparent performance gains when train and test edges share constituent proteins or when negatives are generated in unrealistic ways \parencite{park2012, bernett2024}. The thesis adopts that lesson directly: the Intra benchmark is used with its predefined negatives, the BIOGRID-human benchmark uses sampled non-positive pairs, and cross-dataset comparisons are interpreted cautiously because the label semantics are not identical.

Taken together, these three streams locate the thesis at their intersection. The dissertation is not a review of semiparametric efficiency alone, not a stand-alone benchmark paper on PPI prediction, and not merely a reproduction of recent preprints. Its contribution is to connect prediction-powered estimation, prediction-powered planning, and leakage-aware protein-edge modeling into one coherent inferential workflow, and then to examine where that workflow transfers cleanly and where the protein setting calls for additional caution.

\section{Why Protein-Interaction Prediction Is a Good Test Case}

The protein application is a useful stress test for prediction-powered ideas for three reasons. First, the labels are meaningfully expensive: even curated resources are incomplete and heterogeneous because they summarize many assays, organisms, and validation conditions \parencite{biogrid2019, vidal2011}. Second, the features are naturally multimodal. Protein sequences can be encoded by large protein language models such as ProtTrans or ESM-style models, while textual descriptions from UniProt provide an orthogonal annotation view \parencite{prottrans2022, lin2023, uniprot2025, sentence_bert2019, minilm2020}. Third, the units are not naturally i.i.d. in the strongest sense because two edges may share a protein, which creates a local graph dependence structure that is not part of the standard theory in current prediction-powered power-analysis work. Precisely because the application is statistically awkward, it is useful for testing where the PPI and \texttt{PPI++} theory transfers cleanly and where additional caution or sensitivity analysis is needed.

The application is also a useful reminder that evaluation design is part of the statistical problem. As noted in the related-work section, pair-input prediction is especially vulnerable to leakage and to unrealistic negative construction. In the present study, those concerns become concrete design constraints: the internal Intra dataset comes with predefined negatives, whereas the BIOGRID-human benchmark requires sampled non-positive pairs. For that reason, the thesis treats train/validation/test design as part of the inferential setup rather than as a peripheral preprocessing detail.

\section{Research Questions}

The thesis is organized around four questions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item How should the original PPI and \texttt{PPI++} framework be understood as part of a broader literature on surrogate-assisted inference, measurement error, and semiparametric efficiency?
    \item How should prediction-powered methods be extended when the cheap labels are themselves noisy or surrogate outcomes?
    \item How does predictor quality enter power and sample-size planning in prediction-powered inference?
    \item In protein--protein interaction prediction, can a strong surrogate model be built and then used as the prediction layer for calibrated PPI/\texttt{PPI++} inference under leakage-aware evaluation and shared-protein dependence sensitivity?
\end{enumerate}

\section{Main Contributions}

This thesis makes four main contributions.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item It places the thesis explicitly in the prediction-powered line initiated by \textcite{ppi_framework} and extended by \textcite{ppi_pp}, treating those papers as the main conceptual foundation.
    \item It develops two extension chapters around that foundation: one on efficient correction for noisy surrogate labels \parencite{llmjudge_eif} and one on prediction-powered power analysis \parencite{ppi_power}.
    \item It develops a protein-edge prediction workflow built around processed Intra and BIOGRID edge tables, per-protein sequence and text representations, pairwise feature construction, merged training-pool construction, neural edge predictors, and seed ensembling. The predictive design is informed by the modern literature on sequence-based protein-interaction prediction, protein language modeling, and leakage-aware evaluation \parencite{dscript2021, prottrans2022, lin2023, park2012, bernett2024}.
    \item It uses the frozen best surrogate, a sequence-plus-text ESM2-3B attention ensemble, as the default prediction layer for PPI/\texttt{PPI++} estimation and labeled-budget analysis, and then adds a protein-specific dependence sensitivity analysis to the published i.i.d. power formulas.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Thesis Roadmap}

Chapter~\ref{chap:foundation_llm} summarizes the preprint \emph{Efficient Inference for Noisy LLM-as-a-Judge Evaluation} \parencite{llmjudge_eif} and places it in the broader literature on surrogate-label correction, measurement error, and efficient estimation. Chapter~\ref{chap:foundation_power} summarizes the preprint \emph{Power Analysis for Prediction-Powered Inference} \parencite{ppi_power}, focusing on asymptotic variance, the role of $\lambda$, and labeled-sample planning under an i.i.d. design.

Chapter~\ref{chap:protein_pipeline} then turns to the protein application and describes the predictive workflow used in the study, including its relationship to existing protein-interaction resources, protein language models, and evaluation pitfalls. Chapter~\ref{chap:protein_results} reports predictive results, the prediction-to-inference handoff, PPI/\texttt{PPI++} estimates, power curves, and a dependence-sensitive extension for shared-protein edges. Chapter~\ref{chap:conclusion} concludes with limitations and future directions.
