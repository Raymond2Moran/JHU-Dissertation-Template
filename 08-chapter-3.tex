\chapter{Prior Work II: Power Analysis for Prediction-Powered Inference} \label{chap:foundation_power}

This chapter summarizes the second methodological foundation used in the thesis, the preprint \emph{Power Analysis for Prediction-Powered Inference} \parencite{ppi_power}. Conceptually, it follows Chapters~\ref{chap:introduction} and~\ref{chap:foundation_llm} in a direct sequence: the original PPI and \texttt{PPI++} papers provide the estimator family, the noisy-surrogate chapter clarifies how that family relates to efficient calibration, and this chapter asks the next design-stage question. Once a prediction-powered estimator is fixed, how many labeled observations are required to achieve a desired level of statistical power?

\section{Mean-Type Planning Setup}

The planning problem in \textcite{ppi_power} is stated for a scalar estimand such as a mean or proportion. The starting point is exactly the same one-sample mean estimator family introduced by PPI and \texttt{PPI++}; what changes is that the chapter treats its asymptotic variance as the object to be inverted for design. To match the notation in Chapter~\ref{chap:foundation_llm}, let \(\theta = \mathbb{E}[Y_i]\) be the target parameter, let \(f_i=f(X_i)\) denote the surrogate prediction, let \(L\) be a labeled calibration sample of size \(n\), and let \(U\) be a prediction-only sample of size \(N\). For any quantity \(A_i\), write \(\overline{A}_{U}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i\in U}A_i\) and \(\overline{A}_{L}=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i\in L}A_i\).
For the one-sample mean setting, the basic PPI estimator is
\[
\widehat{\theta}_{\mathrm{PPI}}
=
\overline{f}_{U}
+
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i \in L}(Y_i-f_i),
\]
which is unbiased because $\theta = \mathbb{E}[f_i] + \mathbb{E}[Y_i-f_i]$.

Power-tuned PPI introduces the one-parameter family
\[
\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda}
=
\overline{Y}_{L}
+
\lambda(\overline{f}_{U}-\overline{f}_{L}),
\]
where $\lambda=1$ recovers vanilla PPI. Under the working i.i.d.\ setup of \textcite{ppi_power}, $\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda}$ remains unbiased for any fixed $\lambda$ that is independent of the labeled outcomes. The main design question is therefore not bias correction, but variance reduction.

\subsection{Variance Expansion and the Role of \texorpdfstring{$\lambda$}{lambda}}

Suppressing the unit index inside variance and covariance operators, the large-sample variance of the mean-type PPI/\texttt{PPI++} estimator can be written as
\[
\mathrm{Var}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda}) \approx
\frac{\mathrm{Var}(Y - \lambda f)}{n}
+
\frac{\lambda^2 \mathrm{Var}(f)}{N}.
\]
Expanding the first term gives the equivalent form
\[
\mathrm{Var}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda})
\approx
\frac{\mathrm{Var}(Y)}{n}
+
\lambda^2 \mathrm{Var}(f)\left(\frac{1}{n}+\frac{1}{N}\right)
-
\frac{2\lambda\,\mathrm{Cov}(Y,f)}{n}.
\]
This display makes the role of $\lambda$ concrete. A larger $\lambda$ puts more weight on the prediction-only sample, which can help if $f$ is strongly correlated with $Y$, but can hurt if the added prediction variability dominates the covariance gain. The optimal balance is obtained by minimizing the variance with respect to $\lambda$.

Differentiating the variance expression and setting the derivative to zero yields
\[
\lambda^\star
=
\frac{\mathrm{Cov}(Y,f)}
{\mathrm{Var}(f)\left(1+\frac{n}{N}\right)}.
\]
If the labeled-to-unlabeled ratio is defined as \(r = \frac{n}{N}\), then the oracle tuning parameter can be written more compactly as
\[
\lambda^\star
=
\frac{\mathrm{Cov}(Y,f)}
{\mathrm{Var}(f)(1+r)}.
\]
Substituting $\lambda^\star$ back into the variance formula gives
\[
\mathrm{Var}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda^\star})
\approx
\frac{\mathrm{Var}(Y)}{n}
-
\frac{\mathrm{Cov}(Y,f)^2}{\mathrm{Var}(f)}
\frac{N}{n(n+N)}.
\]
wWriting $\rho_{Yf} = \mathrm{Corr}(Y,f)$ yields the equivalent expression
\[
\mathrm{Var}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda^\star})
\approx
\frac{\mathrm{Var}(Y)}{n}
\left(
1-\frac{\rho_{Yf}^2}{1+r}
\right).
\]
This is the key planning formula in the one-sample mean setting. It shows explicitly that stronger predictions reduce the labeled-sample requirement through the squared correlation between the surrogate and the truth. In that sense, the chapter converts the main qualitative message of PPI and \texttt{PPI++} into a design formula: if better predictions reduce residual variance after calibration, then they should also reduce the number of gold-standard labels needed for a target analysis. When $N$ is much larger than $n$, so that $r \approx 0$, the expression reduces to the familiar rule-of-thumb
\[
\mathrm{Var}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda^\star})
\approx
\frac{\mathrm{Var}(Y)}{n}(1-\rho_{Yf}^2),
\]
which says that the proportional reduction in required labeled sample size is approximately an $R^2$ gain \parencite{ppi_power}. Appendix~\ref{chap:appendix-a} collects the algebra for the variance expansion and the oracle-$\lambda$ minimization.

\section{Connection to the Broader Efficiency Literature}

Classical sample-size calculations typically assume that all usable information comes from the labeled sample itself. The prediction-powered setting is different because a large auxiliary sample with predictions is also available. The statistical question is therefore not just how to size a study under a fixed variance model, but how to translate prediction quality into an explicit reduction in the labeled-sample requirement. That perspective is closely connected to the efficiency-based viewpoint in Chapter~\ref{chap:foundation_llm}: the better the surrogate, the smaller the residual variation that must be learned from expensive labels \parencite{eif_ref, tsiatis2006, ppi_framework}.

The three planning modes emphasized in the preprint are therefore natural rather than ad hoc. Vanilla PPI fixes $\lambda = 1$; estimated-lambda \texttt{PPI++} plugs in a tuning parameter estimated from pilot data; and oracle-lambda \texttt{PPI++} uses the variance-minimizing choice under the working model \parencite{ppi_pp, ppi_power}. These are also the three planning modes used in the thesis codebase and in the Chapter~\ref{chap:protein_results} power figures. The chapter is therefore best understood as the design-stage continuation of \texttt{PPI++} rather than as a separate theory disconnected from the original estimator family.

\section{Wald Inversion and Required Labeled Sample Size}

For a target difference $\delta$ and a two-sided level-$\alpha$ test with desired power $1-\beta$, the planning rule in \textcite{ppi_power} is based on Wald inversion. Consider the one-sample hypothesis
\[
H_0:\theta=\theta_0
\qquad\text{versus}\qquad
H_1:\theta=\theta_0+\delta.
\]
The key object is the target variance scale \(S^2 = \left( \frac{\delta}{z_{1-\alpha/2} + z_{1-\beta}} \right)^2\), and the required labeled sample size is the smallest \(n\) for which the asymptotic variance of the prediction-powered estimator is below this threshold:
\[
\mathrm{Var}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda}) \le S^2.
\]
If $\lambda$ is treated as fixed, this inequality can be rearranged directly to give
\[
n_{\mathrm{req}}(\lambda)
=
\frac{\mathrm{Var}(Y-\lambda f)}
{S^2-\lambda^2\mathrm{Var}(f)/N},
\]
provided the denominator is positive. This expression clarifies the three planning modes used later in the thesis:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item vanilla PPI fixes $\lambda=1$,
    \item estimated-lambda \texttt{PPI++} plugs in a pilot estimate $\widehat{\lambda}$, and
    \item oracle-lambda \texttt{PPI++} uses the variance-minimizing choice $\lambda^\star$.
\end{enumerate}
The oracle case is slightly more delicate because $\lambda^\star$ itself depends on $n$ through the ratio $r=n/N$. The resulting sample-size condition is therefore a quadratic inequality rather than a simple linear inversion. The closed-form solution is given in \textcite{ppi_power}; in the thesis code, the same planning logic is implemented numerically for transparency and flexibility. Additional algebra is collected in Appendix~\ref{chap:appendix-a}.

\section{Assumptions and Scope}

For the protein chapters, one point must be kept explicit: the derivations in \textcite{ppi_power} are published under an i.i.d. sampling design. In particular, the asymptotic variance and labeled-sample formulas assume that the observations used in the power calculation are independent. This is a clean and useful baseline, but it is not automatically guaranteed in graph-structured protein-interaction data, where two edges can share a protein and therefore share information.

Accordingly, this thesis uses the published formulas in two layers:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item as the primary i.i.d. planning baseline, and
    \item as the starting point for a protein-specific dependence sensitivity analysis in Chapter~\ref{chap:protein_results}.
\end{enumerate}

This distinction is important for interpretation. The point of the thesis is not to claim that the existing theory is wrong, but to be explicit about where its assumptions are cleanly satisfied and where a graph-structured application calls for additional caution.

\section{Implications for the Protein Application}

The power-analysis preprint provides the planning layer for the rest of the thesis. Once the protein-interaction prediction workflow yields a strong surrogate, the same framework can be used to answer questions such as: how many experimentally validated edges are needed to estimate a prevalence-like quantity to a desired precision, and how much do label requirements change under vanilla PPI, estimated-lambda \texttt{PPI++}, and oracle-lambda \texttt{PPI++}? Chapter~\ref{chap:protein_results} answers those questions with the frozen best protein surrogate and then extends the discussion to shared-protein dependence.
