\chapter{Foundation II: Power Analysis for Prediction-Powered Inference} \label{chap:foundation_power}

This chapter summarizes the second methodological foundation used in the thesis, the preprint \emph{Power Analysis for Prediction-Powered Inference} \parencite{ppi_power}. The motivating question is simple and practical: once a prediction-powered estimator is available, how many labeled observations are required to achieve a desired level of statistical power?

\section{Mean-Type Planning Setup}

The planning problem in \textcite{ppi_power} is stated for a scalar estimand such as a mean or proportion. Let $Y$ denote the target outcome, let $f(X)$ denote a prediction or surrogate, let $n$ denote the number of labeled observations, and let $N$ denote the number of unlabeled observations with predictions only. For a tuning parameter $\lambda$, the large-sample variance of a mean-type PPI/PPI++ estimator is of the form
\[
\mathrm{Var}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda}) \approx
\frac{\mathrm{Var}(Y - \lambda f)}{n}
+
\frac{\lambda^2 \mathrm{Var}(f)}{N}.
\]
This expression makes the planning intuition transparent. Better predictions reduce the labeled-sample contribution through the residual term $Y - \lambda f$, and a larger prediction-only sample reduces the second term. In that sense, the chapter extends the core logic of prediction-powered inference from point estimation to study design \parencite{ppi_framework, ppi_pp, ppi_power}.

\section{Connection to the Broader Efficiency Literature}

Classical sample-size calculations typically assume that all usable information comes from the labeled sample itself. The prediction-powered setting is different because a large auxiliary sample with predictions is also available. The statistical question is therefore not just how to size a study under a fixed variance model, but how to translate prediction quality into an explicit reduction in the labeled-sample requirement. That perspective is closely connected to the efficiency-based viewpoint in Chapter~\ref{chap:foundation_llm}: the better the surrogate, the smaller the residual variation that must be learned from expensive labels \parencite{eif_ref, ppi_framework}.

The three planning modes emphasized in the preprint are therefore natural rather than ad hoc. Vanilla PPI fixes $\lambda = 1$; estimated-lambda PPI++ plugs in a tuning parameter estimated from pilot data; and oracle-lambda PPI++ uses the variance-minimizing choice under the working model \parencite{ppi_pp, ppi_power}. These are also the three planning modes used in the thesis codebase and in the Chapter~\ref{chap:protein_results} power figures.

\section{Wald Inversion and Required Labeled Sample Size}

For a target difference $\delta$ and a two-sided level-$\alpha$ test with desired power $1-\beta$, the planning rule in \textcite{ppi_power} is based on Wald inversion. In the mean case, the key object is the target variance scale
\[
S^2 = \left( \frac{\delta}{z_{1-\alpha/2} + z_{1-\beta}} \right)^2,
\]
and the required labeled sample size is the smallest $n$ for which the asymptotic variance of the prediction-powered estimator is below this threshold. The practical consequence is that sample-size savings can be tied directly to predictive quality, often through rule-of-thumb relationships that behave like an $R^2$-type gain.

\section{Assumptions and Scope}

For the protein chapters, one point must be kept explicit: the derivations in \textcite{ppi_power} are published under an i.i.d. sampling design. In particular, the asymptotic variance and labeled-sample formulas assume that the observations used in the power calculation are independent. This is a clean and useful baseline, but it is not automatically guaranteed in graph-structured PPI data, where two edges can share a protein and therefore share information.

Accordingly, this thesis uses the published formulas in two layers:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item as the primary i.i.d. planning baseline, and
    \item as the starting point for a protein-specific dependence sensitivity analysis in Chapter~\ref{chap:protein_results}.
\end{enumerate}

This distinction is important for interpretation. The point of the thesis is not to claim that the existing theory is wrong, but to be explicit about where its assumptions are cleanly satisfied and where a graph-structured application calls for additional caution.

\section{Implications for the Protein Application}

The power-analysis preprint provides the planning layer for the rest of the thesis. Once the protein prediction workflow yields a strong surrogate, the same framework can be used to answer questions such as: how many experimentally validated edges are needed to estimate a prevalence-like quantity to a desired precision, and how much do label requirements change under vanilla PPI, estimated-lambda PPI++, and oracle-lambda PPI++? Chapter~\ref{chap:protein_results} answers those questions with the frozen best protein surrogate and then extends the discussion to shared-protein dependence.
