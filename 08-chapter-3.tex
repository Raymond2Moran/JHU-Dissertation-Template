\chapter{Methodological Foundation II: Power Analysis for Prediction-Powered Inference} \label{chap:foundation_power}

This chapter provides the second methodological foundation for the thesis. It builds on the preprint \emph{Power Analysis for Prediction-Powered Inference} \parencite{ppi_power} and takes up the next step in the sequence developed in Chapters~\ref{chap:introduction} and~\ref{chap:foundation_llm}. The original PPI and \texttt{PPI++} papers define the estimator family, and the noisy-surrogate chapter clarifies how that family relates to efficient calibration. The present chapter turns to design: once a prediction-powered estimator has been specified, how many labeled observations are needed to achieve a desired level of statistical power?

For the remainder of the thesis, I use the one-sample i.i.d.\ planning formulas from that paper as the baseline design theory. Later chapters specialize those formulas to the protein application, use pilot \(\rho^2\) as the main planning summary, and then add a dependence-sensitive extension when graph structure makes the i.i.d.\ assumption questionable. A new full power theorem for dependent protein-edge data is outside the scope of this thesis.

\section{Mean-Type Planning Setup}

The planning problem in \textcite{ppi_power} is stated for a scalar estimand such as a mean or proportion. I continue the thesis-wide notation from Section~\ref{sec:global_notation}: \(\theta = \mathbb{E}[Y_i]\) denotes the target parameter, \(f_i=f(X_i)\) denotes the surrogate prediction, \(L\) is the labeled calibration sample of size \(n\), and \(U\) is the large prediction-only sample of size \(N\). The starting point is exactly the same one-sample mean estimator family introduced by PPI and \texttt{PPI++}; what changes is that this chapter treats its asymptotic variance as the object to be inverted for design.

Two additional planning symbols are used repeatedly in this chapter. First, \(\Delta\) denotes a target absolute difference in the mean-type estimand, so the power calculations concern a difference in a mean or proportion rather than PR-AUC or ROC-AUC directly. Second, \(\rho_{Yf} = \Corr(Y,f)\) denotes the surrogate--truth correlation, and \(\rho_{Yf}^2\) is the corresponding coefficient of determination. This quantity is the main summary of surrogate quality in the one-sample planning formulas because it controls how strongly prediction quality reduces the labeled-sample requirement.
For the one-sample mean setting, the basic PPI estimator is
\[
\widehat{\theta}_{\mathrm{PPI}}
=
\overline{f}_{U}
+
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i \in L}(Y_i-f_i),
\]
which is unbiased because $\theta = \mathbb{E}[f_i] + \mathbb{E}[Y_i-f_i]$.

Power-tuned PPI introduces the one-parameter family
\[
\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda}
=
\overline{Y}_{L}
+
\lambda(\overline{f}_{U}-\overline{f}_{L}),
\]
where $\lambda=1$ recovers vanilla PPI. Under the working i.i.d.\ setup of \textcite{ppi_power}, $\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda}$ remains unbiased for any fixed $\lambda$ that is independent of the labeled outcomes. The main design question is therefore not bias correction, but variance reduction.

\subsection{Variance Expansion and the Role of \texorpdfstring{$\lambda$}{lambda}}

Suppressing the unit index inside variance and covariance operators, the large-sample variance of the mean-type PPI/\texttt{PPI++} estimator can be written as
\[
\mathrm{Var}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda}) \approx
\frac{\mathrm{Var}(Y - \lambda f)}{n}
+
\frac{\lambda^2 \mathrm{Var}(f)}{N}.
\]
Expanding the first term gives the equivalent form
\[
\mathrm{Var}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda})
\approx
\frac{\mathrm{Var}(Y)}{n}
+
\lambda^2 \mathrm{Var}(f)\left(\frac{1}{n}+\frac{1}{N}\right)
-
\frac{2\lambda\,\mathrm{Cov}(Y,f)}{n}.
\]
This display makes the role of $\lambda$ concrete. A larger $\lambda$ puts more weight on the prediction-only sample, which can help if $f$ is strongly correlated with $Y$, but can hurt if the added prediction variability dominates the covariance gain. The optimal balance is obtained by minimizing the variance with respect to $\lambda$.

Differentiating the variance expression and setting the derivative to zero yields
\[
\lambda^\star
=
\frac{\mathrm{Cov}(Y,f)}
{\mathrm{Var}(f)\left(1+\frac{n}{N}\right)}.
\]
If the labeled-to-unlabeled ratio is defined as \(r = \frac{n}{N}\), then the oracle tuning parameter can be written more compactly as
\[
\lambda^\star
=
\frac{\mathrm{Cov}(Y,f)}
{\mathrm{Var}(f)(1+r)}.
\]
Substituting $\lambda^\star$ back into the variance formula gives
\[
\mathrm{Var}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda^\star})
\approx
\frac{\mathrm{Var}(Y)}{n}
-
\frac{\mathrm{Cov}(Y,f)^2}{\mathrm{Var}(f)}
\frac{N}{n(n+N)}.
\]
Writing $\rho_{Yf} = \mathrm{Corr}(Y,f)$ yields the equivalent expression
\[
\mathrm{Var}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda^\star})
\approx
\frac{\mathrm{Var}(Y)}{n}
\left(
1-\frac{\rho_{Yf}^2}{1+r}
\right).
\]
This is the key planning formula in the one-sample mean setting. It shows explicitly that stronger predictions reduce the labeled-sample requirement through the squared correlation between the surrogate and the truth. In that sense, the chapter converts the main qualitative message of PPI and \texttt{PPI++} into a design formula: if better predictions reduce residual variance after calibration, then they should also reduce the number of gold-standard labels needed for a target analysis. When $N$ is much larger than $n$, so that $r \approx 0$, the expression reduces to the familiar rule-of-thumb
\[
\mathrm{Var}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda^\star})
\approx
\frac{\mathrm{Var}(Y)}{n}(1-\rho_{Yf}^2),
\]
which says that the proportional reduction in required labeled sample size is approximately an $R^2$ gain \parencite{ppi_power}. Appendix~\ref{chap:appendix-a} collects the algebra for the variance expansion and the oracle-$\lambda$ minimization.

\section{Connection to the Broader Efficiency Literature}

Chapter~\ref{chap:introduction} has already positioned this thesis within the broader prediction-powered and efficiency-based literature. Here the narrower point is the design implication of that perspective. Classical sample-size calculations usually assume that all usable information comes from the labeled sample itself. Prediction-powered planning changes the question because a large auxiliary sample with predictions is also available. The better the surrogate, the smaller the residual variation that must be learned from expensive labels, so surrogate quality should enter the design problem directly rather than only the retrospective analysis \parencite{eif_ref, tsiatis2006, ppi_framework}.

The three planning modes emphasized in the preprint are therefore natural rather than ad hoc. Vanilla PPI fixes $\lambda = 1$; estimated-lambda \texttt{PPI++} plugs in a tuning parameter estimated from pilot data; and oracle-lambda \texttt{PPI++} uses the variance-minimizing choice under the working model \parencite{ppi_pp, ppi_power}. These are also the three planning modes used throughout the later protein analysis. The chapter is therefore best understood as the design-stage continuation of \texttt{PPI++}, not as a separate theory detached from the original estimator family.

\section{Wald Inversion and Required Labeled Sample Size}

For a target difference $\delta$ and a two-sided level-$\alpha$ test with desired power $1-\beta$, the planning rule in \textcite{ppi_power} is based on Wald inversion. Consider the one-sample hypothesis
\[
H_0:\theta=\theta_0
\qquad\text{versus}\qquad
H_1:\theta=\theta_0+\delta.
\]
The key object is the target variance scale \(S^2 = \left( \frac{\delta}{z_{1-\alpha/2} + z_{1-\beta}} \right)^2\), and the required labeled sample size is the smallest \(n\) for which the asymptotic variance of the prediction-powered estimator is below this threshold:
\[
\mathrm{Var}(\widehat{\theta}_{\lambda}) \le S^2.
\]
If $\lambda$ is treated as fixed, this inequality can be rearranged directly to give
\[
n_{\mathrm{req}}(\lambda)
=
\frac{\mathrm{Var}(Y-\lambda f)}
{S^2-\lambda^2\mathrm{Var}(f)/N},
\]
provided the denominator is positive. This expression clarifies the three planning modes used later in the thesis:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item vanilla PPI fixes $\lambda=1$,
    \item estimated-lambda \texttt{PPI++} plugs in a pilot estimate $\widehat{\lambda}$, and
    \item oracle-lambda \texttt{PPI++} uses the variance-minimizing choice $\lambda^\star$.
\end{enumerate}
The oracle case is slightly more delicate because $\lambda^\star$ itself depends on $n$ through the ratio $r=n/N$. The resulting sample-size condition is therefore a quadratic inequality rather than a simple linear inversion. The closed-form solution is given in \textcite{ppi_power}; in the thesis code, the same planning logic is implemented numerically for transparency and flexibility. Additional algebra is collected in Appendix~\ref{chap:appendix-a}.

\section{Real-Data Applications and the Role of Pilot \texorpdfstring{$\rho^2$}{rho2}}

The real-data section of \textcite{ppi_power} is important because it turns the planning formulas into an explicit design workflow rather than leaving them at the level of asymptotic algebra. The paper uses the same structure across all three applications. First, a training split is used to fit the surrogate models. Second, a smaller pilot split is used to estimate the variance and covariance quantities that determine the planning rule, especially $\rho_{Yf}^2$. Third, a large held-out evaluation split is treated as an empirical population on which the planned design can be checked. In each application, the authors allocate $25\%$ of the observed data to training, $15\%$ to the pilot split, and the remaining $60\%$ to held-out evaluation, target power $0.80$ at level $\alpha=0.05$, and estimate achieved power from $500$ held-out resamples. The null value is defined as $\theta_0=\theta_{\mathrm{eval}}-\Delta$, so the evaluation split serves as the practical alternative truth against which the planned labeled sample size is validated.

The original paper presents these applications as a nine-panel figure block with study-overview, planning, and held-out-power panels. For the thesis, Figure~\ref{fig:chap3_real_data_planning} reproduces only the planning panels, because they are the part that connects most directly to the sample-size formulas used later in the protein application.

\paragraph{Baron pancreas scRNA-seq.}
The first case study uses the pancreas single-cell RNA-seq atlas of \textcite{baron2016single}. The paper restricts attention to alpha and beta cells and studies the composition contrast \(\theta = p_{\beta}-p_{\alpha}\), so the target is a difference in cell-type proportions rather than a classifier metric. Among the \(4{,}851\) beta-or-alpha cells retained for the application, \(1{,}212\) are assigned to training, \(727\) to the pilot split, and \(2{,}912\) to evaluation. In the evaluation subset, \(p_{\beta}=0.521\) and \(p_{\alpha}=0.479\), giving \(\theta_{\mathrm{eval}}=0.041\). The paper then constructs a deliberate gradient of surrogate quality: a weak donor-only logistic model, a one-gene logistic model based on \texttt{INS} expression, and a random forest fit on the top 20 marker genes identified within the training split. This produces a wide spread in pilot \(\rho_{Yf}^2\), from about \(0.14\) for the donor-only model to about \(0.97\) and \(0.98\) for the stronger expression-based surrogates. The planning consequence is immediate. For \(\Delta=0.10\) and \(N=500\), the classical design requires \(n_{\mathrm{cl}}=785\) labeled cells, the weak surrogate reduces that only slightly to \(n^\star=740\), and the two strong surrogates reduce it sharply to \(325\) and \(306\) labeled cells, with held-out powers still close to the nominal target at about \(0.83\) to \(0.84\). This is the paper's clearest example of a high-\(\rho^2\) regime in which prediction-powered planning materially changes the design.

\paragraph{NHANES systolic blood pressure.}
The second application uses NHANES 2017--2018 data \parencite{nhanes2020} and treats mean systolic blood pressure as the estimand of interest. After merging the demographic, anthropometric, and examination files, the paper retains \(4{,}990\) adults with complete covariates and at least one systolic blood-pressure measurement. The outcome is the mean systolic blood pressure \(Y\), and the predictors include age, sex, race, body-mass index, and waist circumference. The split is \(1{,}247\) training observations, \(748\) pilot observations, and \(2{,}995\) evaluation observations, with evaluation mean \(\theta_{\mathrm{eval}}=125.9\) mmHg. Three surrogates are compared: an age-only linear model, a richer clinical linear model, and a clinical random forest. Here the pilot \(\rho_{Yf}^2\) values are much more moderate, around \(0.22\) to \(0.26\), so the design gains are correspondingly moderate. For a target shift of \(\Delta=4\) mmHg and \(N=2{,}000\), the classical design requires \(187\) labeled patients, while the three surrogate-based designs require \(149\), \(144\), and \(142\) labeled patients, with held-out power between \(0.81\) and \(0.85\). The example is useful because it shows a middle regime: the prediction-powered gain is real, but the gain is incremental rather than transformative when pilot \(\rho^2\) is only modest.

\paragraph{ISIC melanoma prevalence.}
The third application uses the ISIC 2020 dermoscopy challenge data \parencite{rotemberg2021patient}. This example shifts from a continuous outcome back to a rare binary outcome and is therefore especially relevant to the classification settings considered later in the thesis. The dataset contains \(33{,}126\) images from \(2{,}056\) patients, with melanoma prevalence about \(1.8\%\). Because multiple images can come from the same patient, the split is performed at the patient level to avoid leakage across repeated lesions. The resulting image counts are \(8{,}609\) for training, \(5{,}090\) for the pilot split, and \(19{,}427\) for evaluation, corresponding to \(514\), \(308\), and \(1{,}234\) patients, respectively. The evaluation prevalence is \(\theta_{\mathrm{eval}}=0.018\). The paper compares a metadata-only logistic model, a logistic model based on principal components of thumbnail images, and a logistic model fit on CLIP image embeddings. On the held-out evaluation split, their AUROCs are about \(0.72\), \(0.79\), and \(0.83\), so the image-based surrogates do discriminate better than the metadata baseline. Yet the planning gains remain very small. For \(\Delta=0.01\) and \(N=10{,}000\), the classical design requires \(1{,}334\) labeled images, while the three surrogate-based designs require \(1{,}299\), \(1{,}310\), and \(1{,}296\) labeled images. The reason is that pilot \(\rho_{Yf}^2\) stays below about \(0.05\) for all three models. In other words, the application shows that visibly better discrimination does not automatically translate into large prediction-powered gains when the estimand is a rare prevalence.

Taken together, the three applications define three distinct planning regimes. The Baron example is a high-\(\rho^2\) regime in which strong surrogates substantially reduce the labeled-sample requirement. The NHANES example is a moderate-\(\rho^2\) regime in which the gains are genuine but measured. The ISIC example is a low-\(\rho^2\) regime in which even improved classifier discrimination leaves the design close to classical. That comparison matters for the thesis because it identifies the right planning quantity. Generic predictive summaries such as AUROC or PR-AUC are not, by themselves, the driver of prediction-powered sample-size savings. In the one-sample mean-type setting, the design gain is governed by agreement between truth and surrogate on the pilot split, summarized here by \(\rho_{Yf}^2\). Chapter~\ref{chap:protein_results} carries exactly that lesson into the protein application by comparing frozen surrogates through their pilot \(\rho^2\) values and then translating those values into required labeled sample sizes.

\section{Assumptions and Scope}

For the protein chapters, one point must be kept explicit: the derivations in \textcite{ppi_power} are published under an i.i.d. sampling design. In particular, the asymptotic variance and labeled-sample formulas assume that the observations used in the power calculation are independent. This is a clean and useful baseline, but it is not automatically guaranteed in graph-structured protein-interaction data, where two edges can share a protein and therefore share information.

Accordingly, this thesis uses the published formulas in two layers:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item as the primary i.i.d. planning baseline, and
    \item as the starting point for a protein-specific dependence sensitivity analysis in Chapter~\ref{chap:protein_results}.
\end{enumerate}

This distinction is important for interpretation. The point of the thesis is not to claim that the existing theory is wrong, but to be explicit about where its assumptions are cleanly satisfied and where a graph-structured application calls for additional caution.

\section{Implications for the Protein Application}

The power-analysis preprint provides the planning layer for the rest of the thesis. Once the protein-interaction prediction workflow yields a strong surrogate, the same framework can be used to answer questions such as: how many experimentally validated edges are needed to estimate a prevalence-like quantity to a desired precision, and how much do label requirements change under vanilla PPI, estimated-lambda \texttt{PPI++}, and oracle-lambda \texttt{PPI++}? Chapter~\ref{chap:protein_results} answers those questions with the frozen best protein surrogate and then extends the discussion to shared-protein dependence.
