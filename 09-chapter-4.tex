\chapter{Protein-PPI Extension: Data, Features, and Predictive Pipeline} \label{chap:protein_pipeline}

This chapter describes the actual predictive workflow implemented in the thesis repository and situates it within the existing literature on PPI resources, sequence-based prediction, protein language models, and evaluation design. The mainline pipeline is the neural attention-ensemble branch, not the earlier baseline-only benchmark path. The modeling story therefore proceeds from processed edge tables to per-protein embeddings, pairwise feature construction, merged training-pool design, neural edge prediction, and seed ensembling.

\section{Data Sources, Curation, and Split Construction}

Two datasets are used in the protein application.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Intra}: the internal benchmark with train, validation, and test partitions used for model selection and main in-domain evaluation.
    \item \textbf{BIOGRID human physical interactions}: an external robustness dataset used to test transportability under a protein-disjoint split.
\end{enumerate}

This design is motivated by the broader literature on interactome construction. BioGRID is one of the major curated resources for experimentally observed interactions, whereas the human reference interactome and related mapping projects illustrate both the value and incompleteness of current gold-standard PPI catalogs \parencite{biogrid2019, luck2020, vidal2011}. The processed edge tables in this thesis are created by \texttt{01\_prepare\_intra\_data.py} and \texttt{02\_prepare\_biogrid\_human.py}. The mainline pipeline keeps validation and test edges isolated from training, and the external BIOGRID split is constructed to reduce leakage through shared proteins.

That split policy is not merely a software convenience. Pair-input prediction tasks are known to be sensitive to optimistic evaluation when constituent objects reappear across train and test or when negative examples are generated in a way that does not reflect the real deployment target \parencite{park2012, bernett2024}. For PPI prediction in particular, those issues can substantially inflate reported performance. The thesis therefore treats leakage control as part of the statistical design of the benchmark rather than as an afterthought.

\section{Per-Protein Embeddings and Pair Features}

The pipeline builds two views for each protein.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Sequence view}: embeddings from pretrained protein language models, including ProtBERT and ESM2 variants.
    \item \textbf{Text view}: embeddings from UniProt description text using a compact sentence-transformer backbone.
\end{enumerate}

The sequence side of the workflow is motivated by the recent success of large self-supervised protein representation models. ProtTrans demonstrated that language-model pretraining on large protein corpora yields embeddings that transfer across many downstream tasks, while the ESM line of work showed that large protein language models can encode rich structural and functional information \parencite{prottrans2022, lin2023}. The text side is complementary rather than redundant: UniProt descriptions encode curated biological context that is not present in raw sequence alone \parencite{uniprot2025}. To represent those descriptions, the thesis uses sentence-embedding models descended from Sentence-BERT and MiniLM \parencite{sentence_bert2019, minilm2020}.

The feature-building stage converts protein-level embeddings into pair-level representations. For each protein pair $(u,v)$, the repository constructs operator-based features,
\[
[u+v,\; |u-v|,\; u \odot v,\; \cos(u,v)],
\]
for the sequence block, the text block, or their concatenation. These are generated by \texttt{05\_build\_pair\_features.py}. The resulting feature sets are \texttt{seq}, \texttt{text}, and \texttt{seq\_text}. This operator-based construction is a pragmatic compromise: it is simple enough to support strong baselines and uncertainty analysis, but expressive enough to let the downstream neural heads learn nonlinear edge structure.

\section{Relationship to the PPI Prediction Literature}

The predictive design in this thesis is related to, but not identical with, the recent literature on sequence-based PPI prediction. Earlier neural approaches already showed that amino-acid sequence alone can yield useful interaction predictors \parencite{sun2017}. Later models such as D-SCRIPT emphasized cross-species transfer, interpretability, and the value of sequence-driven inductive bias \parencite{dscript2021}. More recent methods have combined deep architectures with protein language model features, as illustrated by xCAPT5 and the broader review literature on deep PPI analysis \parencite{xcapt52024, lee2023}.

The current thesis does not aim to declare a universal state of the art against those papers, because direct metric comparison is difficult when datasets, negative-sampling protocols, and split designs differ. Instead, it adopts the relevant methodological lesson from that literature: strong single-protein representations matter, but evaluation design matters just as much \parencite{park2012, bernett2024}. That is why the thesis prioritizes leakage-aware splits and external robustness checks over maximizing performance on a single convenient benchmark.

\section{Merged Training Pool and Neural Predictors}

The mainline training branch uses a merged leakage-controlled training pool. The script \texttt{09\_build\_training\_pool.py} combines the available training edges from the Intra and BIOGRID tables, removes any pair that overlaps with downstream holdout sets, and collapses duplicates with a positive-wins rule. This produces a larger training pool while preserving strict separation from validation and test edges.

Three neural prediction heads are implemented in \texttt{06\_train\_models\_nn.py}.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{NN-LogReg}: a linear head on top of the pair features.
    \item \textbf{NN-MLP}: a multilayer perceptron with nonlinear hidden layers.
    \item \textbf{NN-Attn}: a multihead self-attention model over operator/modality tokens.
\end{enumerate}

For the \texttt{seq\_text} setting, the attention model does not treat the full feature vector as an undifferentiated block. Instead, it splits the pair representation into sequence and text operator tokens, projects those tokens into a shared latent space, applies multihead self-attention, and then predicts one edge-level probability. The final prediction used in this thesis is a three-seed ensemble of the best attention configuration. This architecture choice is deliberately modest relative to full end-to-end cross-encoder designs: the aim is to obtain a strong and stable surrogate that can be exported cleanly into the downstream inference pipeline.

\section{Frozen Best Model and Prediction Handoff}

The frozen best surrogate in the current repository is:
\begin{center}
\texttt{seq\_text + esm2\_3b + attention ensemble}.
\end{center}
It is built by training three attention models with different random seeds and averaging their predicted probabilities using \texttt{10\_ensemble\_predictions.py}. The resulting ensemble achieves the best observed test-set performance in the current workspace, with PR-AUC 0.715 on the Intra test split and PR-AUC 0.799 on the BIOGRID-human test split.

The predictive and inferential layers are connected through an explicit export step. Per-run training outputs are stored in each run directory as \texttt{predictions.csv}. The ensemble predictions are then converted to a canonical export schema by \texttt{07\_export\_predictions.py}. All downstream R scripts read that canonical export rather than directly reading model-specific training artifacts. This separation is important for reproducibility: training, ensembling, export, and inference are distinct stages with distinct output contracts.

\section{Chapter Summary}

The protein application therefore uses a script-first predictive pipeline whose mainline is the merged-pool attention-ensemble branch. The scientific context for that design comes from curated PPI resources, protein language modeling, and the recent literature on leakage-aware sequence-based PPI prediction \parencite{biogrid2019, luck2020, prottrans2022, dscript2021, park2012, bernett2024}. This chapter establishes the prediction layer. The next chapter evaluates that layer, then uses its exported predictions as inputs to PPI/PPI++ estimation, labeled-budget planning, and dependence sensitivity analysis.
